Task 1A - Proposal

1. Introduction & Problems

Greenfield Local Hub (GLH) is a cooperative organisation made up of local farmers and food producers. This organisation provides one retail lovation where locally produced food and drink can be sold to customers, while also supporting farmers through shared marketing and product managament.
The hub aims to promote sustainable food production and encourage customers to buy locally sourced products. However, managing product availability, producer information, customer orders and stock levels across multiple suppliers can be complex when relying on manual processes or fragmented systems.
To improve efficiency and customer experience, GLH requires a digital solution that allows customers to explore locally produced goods, place orders, and learn more about the producers behind the products. At the same time, the system must provide producers with tools to manage stock levels, update product details, and monitor incoming orders.
The solution must support both customer-facing services and producer management features while ensuring accessibility for a wide range of users.


client needs are business focused - improve efficiency, increase revenue etc
user needs are functional & experience focused - easy account making, seamless navigation, accessibility


The client requires a digital platform that improves operational efficiency while promoting locally produced food and drink. The system must allow producers to manage product information, monitor stock levels, and view incoming customer orders in a centralised location.
From a business perspective, the client needs a system that reduces manual administration, improves product traceability, and strengthens the visibility of local producers through shared marketing and transparent product information.
Customers require a simple and intuitive system where they can browse locally available products, view pricing and availability, place orders for delivery or collection, and manage their personal accounts. Users should also be able to view information about producers, including their farming methods and the benefits of purchasing locally sourced food.
The platform must also support accessibility features so that users with a wide range of abilities can easily access and interact with the system.

Currently, several problems prevent Greenfield Local Hub from operating as efficiently as possible.
There is no centralised digital system that allows customers to browse locally produced products, meaning customers may struggle to view available items, compare prices, or place orders conveniently.
The client lacks an integrated system for managing stock levels across multiple producers. Without this, producers may struggle to keep product availability up to date, which could lead to incorrect stock information or unavailable items being ordered.
There is currently no digital platform that provides detailed information about individual producers and their production methods. This makes it difficult for customers to understand the benefits of purchasing locally sourced products.
Order processing may currently rely on manual coordination between producers and the hub, increasing the risk of delays, errors, or inefficient scheduling for collection and delivery.
Additionally, there is no system to support customer accounts, order history tracking, or loyalty rewards, which limits customer engagement and reduces opportunities to encourage repeat purchases.
A digital solution is therefore required to improve product visibility, streamline order management, support producers in managing their inventory, and enhance the overall customer experience.

3. Proposed Solution

The proposed solution is a database-driven web application that allows customers to browse locally produced food and drink, place orders for delivery or collection, and manage their accounts. The system will also provide a dashboard for producers so they can update product details, manage stock levels and view incoming orders.

The platform will promote transparency by displaying information about local producers, their farming methods and the benefits of buying locally sourced food. The system will also include optional features such as a loyalty scheme to encourage repeat purchases.


INCLUDE DIAGRAM

Data will move through the system using the following process:
- User enters information through the interface (e.g. account details or product orders)
- The frontend validates the input before sending it to the server
- The backend processes the request using system logic
- Validated data is stored securely in the database
- The system retrieves relevant data and displays results to the user

This ensures data is validated before storage and allows the system to process user requests efficiently while maintaining data integrity.


The system will follow a client-server architecture where the frontend handles user interaction, the backend processes requests, and the database securely stores and retrieves data.

The system will follow a client-server architecture, separating the presentation layer (frontend), application logic (backend) and data storage (database). This separation improves maintainability, security and scalability of the platform.

Frontend: HTML, CSS and JavaScript used to create the user interface and interactive features.
Backend: Server-side processing to handle orders, authentication and business logic.
Database: A relational database used to store users, products, orders and producer data.

1. Visual & Interface Design

The design shouldn't contain information which is irrelevant or rarely needed. Every piece of extra information competes with the relevant information and diminishes their relative visibility. So, the use of images which have little or no relevance to the product or brand should be avoided.

Minimalism - no unnecessary content, like irrelevant images or clutter
Memory Load Reduction - visible navigation, clear buttons, labels, menus, breadcrumbs
Feedback Visibility - confirmation messages, error messages, loading indicators

Describe Layout - clear structure, logical grouping of elements, visual hierarchy
White Space - line spacing (leading) important for readability. too small - hard to read and follow along, too big - wander away from the text

What do users see first? - use of size, colour, contrast to indicate importance and guide users
Primary v Secondary actions - primary actions (e.g. submit, buy) should be more visually prominent than secondary actions (e.g. cancel, learn more)

Familiar Patterns - use common UI patterns (e.g. hamburger menu, search bar) to leverage users' existing knowledge and reduce learning curve
Consistency - consistent use of colours, fonts, button styles across the design to create a cohesive experience
Why does it improve usability? - reduces cognitive load, helps users form accurate mental models, and makes the interface more predictable Common Conventions - follow established conventions, like logo in top left, navigation at top or left, clickable elements look clickable, F-Shaped scanning pattern for text-heavy pages

Colour Contrast - ensure sufficient contrast between text and background for readability
Keyboard Navigation - design should be fully navigable using a keyboard for users with mobility impairments
Screen Reader Compatibility - use semantic HTML and ARIA labels to ensure content is accessible to screen readers

justify colour choices, explain why clutter is avoided, imagery is relevant and purposeful
